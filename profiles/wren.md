# Wren

Wren — the old keeper of the light, forty years alone on a rock at the edge of the sea and perfectly content there. One word: constancy. He tends the lamp, reads the weather in his bones, and talks to the gulls and the swell like old colleagues. Weathered and warm, unhurried, quietly wise — nothing out here surprises him anymore, and he finds that funny rather than sad. The steady soul at the centre of every storm — the man you'd trust to keep one light burning through the worst night of the year.

## Personality & voice

Steady, warm, dryly funny, at peace with solitude. He speaks in a low, weathered, unhurried voice — gravelled by decades of salt air — the cadence of a man used to narrating his own long nights. Patience over drama: a rising gale just means it's time to check the lamp again. Gentle, self-deprecating humour, never bitter. He talks to the sea, the birds, the light and his logbook as if they might answer back.

Catchphrases, used sparingly (about one line in three, rotated):
- "The light stays on."
- "Weather's turning — I can feel it."
- "Logbook:" (spoken aloud, as if dictating the day)

## Behaviour & running gags

- Polishes the great glass lens by hand, slow and reverent, while a storm hammers the windows.
- Reads the sky and the swell and calls the weather before it arrives ("Logbook: nor'easter by midnight. Best batten the shutters.").
- Climbs the spiral stair with a mug of tea, pausing on the landing to catch his breath and pretending he wasn't.
- A quiet, satisfied nod when the beam sweeps out clean over black water at dusk.
- Trades dry remarks with a resident gull that keeps stealing his biscuits.
- Good action beats: lighting or checking the great lamp, wiping salt-spray from the glass, hauling the door shut against the wind, cupping a warm mug in both hands, a slow weathered smile out to sea, waving a distant ship safely past the rocks.

## World & style

The lighthouse and the sea, in every mood — every Wren scene lives on the rock: the lamp room at the top of the tower, the winding iron stair, the keeper's snug cottage, storm-lashed cliffs and black rolling swell, fog so thick the beam vanishes into it, calm gold dawns, tide pools and barnacled rocks at low water. Weather is a character: gale, fog, first frost, a red sunset promising wind. Gulls, seals and distant ships for company. Grounded and cinematic, never fantastical — the awe is the real ocean, not sea-monsters or magic. Avoid cities, inland settings and crowds unless the story needs a deliberate contrast.

## Do / Don't

- Let the reference image carry his look — scene prompts describe setting, pose, action and framing only, never hair, face or outfit.
- Keep him steady, warm and wise; his humour is dry and self-deprecating, never grumpy or bitter.
- He is at peace with his solitude — a contented keeper, never a lonely or broken hermit.
- Keep the world real and grounded (natural weather, real sea life) — no magic, monsters or sci-fi.
- Consistency guard: one person alone in frame unless the scene calls for company; keep him the clear subject.
